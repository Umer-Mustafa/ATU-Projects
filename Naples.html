Teatro Di San Carlo

Teatro Di San Carlo (Royal Theatre of Saint Charles),. is an opera house in Naples and is connected to the Royal Palace and adjacent to the Piazza Del Plebiscito. It is the oldest continuously active venue for opera in the world, having opened in 1737, decades before either Milan's La Scala or Venice's La Fenice. The opera season runs from late January to May, with the ballet season taking place from April to early June. The house had a seating capacity of 3,285 but this has been reduced to 1,386 seats. Given the size, structure and antiquity, it was the model for theatres that were later built in Europe.

Royal Palace of Naples

Palazzo Reale di Napoli (Royal Palace of Naples) is a palace, museum, and historical tourist destination located in central Naples, southern Italy. Construction on the present building was begun in the 17th century by the architect Domenico Fontana. By 1616, the facade had been completed, and by 1620, the interior was frescoed by Battistello Caracciolo, Giovanni Balducci, and Belisario Corenzio. The decoration of the Royal Chapel of Assumption was not completed until 1644 by Antonio Picchiatti. The Palace is also connected to the Teatro Di San Carlo and the Piazza Del Plebiscito.

Castel Nuovo

Castel Nuovo, often called Maschio Angioino built in 1282 , is a medieval castle located in front of Piazza Municipio and the city hall (Palazzo San Giacomo) in central Naples. Its scenic location and imposing size makes the castle, one of the main architectural landmarks of the city. It is the headquarters of Neapolitan Society of Homeland History and of the Naples Committee of the Institute for the History of the Italian Risorgimento. In the complex there is also the civic museum, which includes the Palatine Chapel and the museum paths on the first and second floors.

Castel dell'Ovo

Castel dell'Ovo is a seafront castle in Naples, located on the former island of Megaride, now a peninsula, on the Gulf of Naples in Italy and is the oldest standing fortification in Naples. The castle's name comes from a legend about the Roman poet Virgil, who had a reputation in the Middle Ages as a great sorcerer and predictor of the future. In the legend, Virgil put a magical egg into the foundations to support the fortifications. It remains there along with his bones, and had this egg been broken, the castle would have been destroyed, and a series of disastrous events for Naples would have followed.
